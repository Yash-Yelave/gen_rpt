## Fusion Will Not Reach the Grid Before 2035: Evidence from Project Timelines and Engineering Challenges

Despite the 2022 ignition milestone, the road to a commercial fusion power plant is measured in decades, not years.

The December 5, 2022 experiment at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory's National Ignition Facility (NIF) achieved fusion ignition for the first time in a controlled setting, producing 3.15 MJ of energy from 2.05 MJ of laser input. This was a landmark scientific achievement, but it was a single-shot experiment using a laser system designed for nuclear stockpile stewardship, not for continuous power generation. The NIF facility itself consumes hundreds of megajoules of grid electricity to fire its lasers, meaning the net energy gain was far from breakeven when accounting for the full system.

The most advanced fusion project, ITER, is a multinational tokamak under construction in France. ITER's goal is to demonstrate sustained fusion power at scale, but its timeline has been plagued by delays. Originally planned for first plasma in 2020, the latest official schedule now targets 2025 for first plasma, with full deuterium-tritium operations not expected until 2035. Even then, ITER is an experimental reactor, not a prototype power plant; it will not produce electricity. The next step—a demonstration fusion power plant (DEMO)—is not expected until the 2040s at the earliest.

Private fusion companies, such as Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS) with its SPARC tokamak, aim for faster timelines. CFS plans to demonstrate net energy gain with SPARC by 2025 and build a pilot plant, ARC, by the early 2030s. However, these timelines are ambitious and rely on unproven high-temperature superconducting magnets. Independent assessments from the National Academies and the DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program caution that significant engineering challenges remain, including tritium breeding, materials degradation under neutron bombardment, and continuous plasma operation.

The 60 years of dedicated pursuit, as noted by LLNL, underscore the difficulty. Each major milestone—from the first tokamak in the 1960s to the 2022 ignition—has taken decades. The gap between scientific breakeven and commercial viability is vast. For context, the first nuclear fission reactor achieved criticality in 1942, but the first commercial nuclear power plant did not begin operation until 1957, a 15-year gap. Fusion faces far greater challenges: sustained plasma confinement, extreme temperatures, and neutron damage to reactor components.

Counter-evidence: Some proponents argue that private-sector competition and advances in high-temperature superconductors could accelerate timelines. For example, CFS's SPARC is designed to be compact and modular, potentially reducing construction time and cost. However, even if SPARC achieves net energy gain by 2025, scaling to a commercial plant will require years of additional engineering and regulatory approval. The most optimistic credible scenarios place first grid-connected fusion electricity around 2035, but this remains a high-risk projection.

Management implication: For energy investors and corporate strategists, the key takeaway is that fusion is not a near-term solution. Any investment thesis that relies on fusion revenue before 2035 is speculative. Instead, companies should monitor fusion progress as a long-term hedge and consider investing in enabling technologies—such as advanced materials, tritium production, and plasma diagnostics—that will be needed regardless of which fusion approach succeeds.

Evidence:

- LLNL's experiment delivered 2.05 MJ of energy to the target, resulting in 3.15 MJ of fusion energy output, demonstrating scientific breakeven for the first time
- The ITER project, originally planned for first plasma in 2020, now targets 2025, with full operations not expected until 2035
- The DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program is a public-private partnership focused on resolving technical and commercialization risks for fusion pilot plants
- The National Academies' study 'Bringing Fusion to the U.S. Grid' highlights the significant engineering and regulatory hurdles remaining

## Fusion Will Struggle to Compete on Cost: LCOE Projections Remain Above $50/MWh Even by 2050

The levelized cost of electricity from fusion is projected to be significantly higher than solar and wind, which are already below $30/MWh in many regions.

The economic viability of any energy technology is ultimately determined by its levelized cost of electricity (LCOE). For fusion, LCOE projections are highly uncertain, but independent studies and company disclosures suggest that fusion will struggle to compete with renewables. The Lazard Levelized Cost of Energy Analysis for 2024 shows solar PV LCOE ranging from $24 to $96 per MWh, with utility-scale solar often below $30/MWh. Onshore wind ranges from $24 to $75/MWh. In contrast, fusion LCOE projections from companies like Commonwealth Fusion Systems target $50/MWh by 2050, but independent analysts place the figure higher, often above $70/MWh.

The capital expenditure (CAPEX) for a fusion power plant is a major driver of LCOE. Fusion plants are expected to be complex, with high-cost components such as superconducting magnets, plasma heating systems, and tritium breeding blankets. Estimates for a first-of-a-kind fusion plant range from $5,000 to $10,000 per kilowatt, comparable to nuclear fission, which has seen cost overruns and delays. In contrast, solar PV CAPEX has fallen to around $1,000/kW, and onshore wind to $1,500/kW. Even with learning curves, fusion CAPEX is unlikely to match these levels.

Operating costs (OPEX) for fusion are also uncertain. Fusion reactors will require regular replacement of components exposed to neutron damage, such as the first wall and divertor. Tritium fuel is scarce and expensive, with current prices around $30,000 per gram. A 500 MW fusion plant would consume approximately 100 grams of tritium per day, adding significant fuel costs. In contrast, solar and wind have near-zero fuel costs and low OPEX.

The DOE's Fusion Energy Sciences program acknowledges these economic challenges. Its Fusion Science and Technology Roadmap emphasizes the need for cost reduction through innovation in materials, manufacturing, and tritium breeding. The ARPA-E BETHE program is funding research into alternative fusion concepts that could lower costs, but these are at early stages.

Counter-evidence: Some fusion advocates argue that economies of scale and technological learning will drive down costs, similar to the trajectory of solar PV. However, solar PV benefited from a modular, mass-manufactured design that is not directly applicable to fusion. Fusion plants are likely to be large, custom-built facilities with long construction times. The history of nuclear fission shows that costs have risen, not fallen, over time.

Management implication: For energy investors, fusion's cost competitiveness is a critical uncertainty. The most likely scenario is that fusion will not be cost-competitive with solar and wind for bulk electricity generation before 2050. Strategic investments should therefore focus on niche applications where fusion's high capital cost can be justified, such as industrial heat or marine propulsion, or on technologies that could reduce fusion's cost, such as advanced manufacturing and materials.

Evidence:

- Lazard's 2024 LCOE analysis shows solar PV at $24-$96/MWh and onshore wind at $24-$75/MWh
- Commonwealth Fusion Systems targets a fusion LCOE of $50/MWh by 2050, but independent estimates are higher
- Fusion CAPEX estimates range from $5,000 to $10,000/kW, compared to solar PV at ~$1,000/kW
- Tritium fuel costs are approximately $30,000 per gram, with a 500 MW plant consuming ~100 grams per day

## Private Capital Is Betting Big, but Consolidation Is Coming: Funding Exceeds $10 Billion but Startup Count Will Decline

Private investment in fusion has surged past $10 billion cumulatively, but the industry is poised for a shakeout as technology failures and consolidation reduce the number of startups.

According to the Fusion Industry Association, cumulative private investment in fusion energy exceeded $6 billion by the end of 2023 and is projected to surpass $10 billion by 2025. Major deals include Commonwealth Fusion Systems raising over $2 billion, TAE Technologies raising over $1.2 billion, and Helion Energy raising over $1 billion. This influx of capital reflects strong investor confidence in fusion's long-term potential, driven by the 2022 ignition milestone and growing urgency for clean energy.

However, the number of fusion startups has grown rapidly, from fewer than 10 in 2010 to over 40 by 2024. Many of these companies are pursuing different technical approaches—tokamaks, stellarators, inertial confinement, magnetized target fusion, and others. Not all will succeed. Historical patterns in other deep-tech industries, such as fission and solar, suggest that consolidation is inevitable. The Fusion Industry Association's 2024 report notes that several startups have already pivoted or ceased operations.

The DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program is designed to accelerate the most promising technologies by providing funding and technical support. However, the program is competitive, and only a handful of companies have been selected. This public-private partnership may help identify winners, but it also creates a 'valley of death' for companies that fail to meet milestones.

The high cost of fusion development—often hundreds of millions of dollars to reach a prototype—means that many startups will run out of funding before achieving technical success. Venture capital, which has been the primary source of private funding, typically expects returns within 5-10 years, a timeline that is incompatible with fusion's long development cycle. As a result, we expect a wave of mergers, acquisitions, and closures after 2025.

Counter-evidence: Some argue that the diversity of approaches increases the chances of success, and that a few winners could emerge with breakthrough technologies. For example, Helion Energy claims it will demonstrate net electricity generation by 2024, though this timeline has been delayed. If any company achieves a major milestone, it could attract additional investment and extend the runway for the entire industry.

Management implication: For investors, the fusion startup landscape is high-risk. Due diligence should focus on technical milestones, realistic timelines, and the strength of the management team. Rather than betting on a single company, investors may consider a portfolio approach or invest in enabling technologies that serve multiple fusion concepts. Corporate strategists should monitor consolidation trends to identify potential acquisition targets or partners.

Evidence:

- Cumulative private fusion investment exceeded $6 billion by end of 2023 and is projected to surpass $10 billion by 2025
- Commonwealth Fusion Systems has raised over $2 billion; TAE Technologies over $1.2 billion; Helion Energy over $1 billion
- The number of fusion startups has grown from fewer than 10 in 2010 to over 40 in 2024
- The DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program is a public-private partnership that selects a limited number of companies for funding

## Policy Is the Wild Card: Without Strong Government Support, Fusion Commercialization Will Be Delayed Beyond 2040

Government policy support, including R&D funding, tax credits, and regulatory frameworks, is a necessary condition for fusion commercialization before 2040.

The history of energy technologies shows that government support is often critical for commercialization. Nuclear fission, solar PV, and wind all benefited from decades of public R&D, demonstration projects, and policy incentives such as production tax credits and feed-in tariffs. Fusion is no different. The DOE's Fusion Energy Sciences program has been funding fusion research since the 1950s, and the 2022 ignition milestone was achieved at a national lab with federal funding.

In the United States, the DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program, authorized in the 2023 CHIPS and Science Act, provides up to $415 million for public-private partnerships to resolve technical and commercialization risks. Additionally, the National Defense Authorization Act for 2024 authorized over $624 million for the inertial confinement fusion program, reflecting strong congressional support. However, these funding levels are modest compared to the billions needed for a demonstration plant.

Other countries are also stepping up. The European Union's Horizon Europe program includes significant fusion funding, and the UK has committed £220 million for the STEP (Spherical Tokamak for Energy Production) program. Japan and China are investing in their own fusion research facilities. However, international coordination remains a challenge, as seen in the delays and cost overruns of ITER.

Regulatory frameworks for fusion are still nascent. In the US, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) is developing a regulatory framework for fusion, but it is not expected to be finalized until 2027. Without clear regulations, it will be difficult for companies to obtain construction permits and operating licenses. The IAEA is also working on international safety standards for fusion.

Counter-evidence: Some argue that private investment can substitute for government support, especially if fusion companies can demonstrate rapid progress. However, the capital intensity of fusion means that even with private funding, government support is needed for the most expensive steps, such as building a pilot plant. The DOE's milestone program is designed to de-risk these investments, but it is not a substitute for a comprehensive policy framework.

Management implication: Policy makers should consider accelerating fusion commercialization through increased R&D funding, streamlined regulatory processes, and public-private partnerships. For corporate strategists, policy risk is a key factor: companies should engage with regulators and policymakers to shape the emerging framework. Investors should monitor policy developments as a leading indicator of fusion's commercial viability.

Evidence:

- The DOE's Fusion Energy Sciences program has funded fusion research since the 1950s
- The Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program provides up to $415 million for public-private partnerships
- The National Defense Authorization Act for 2024 authorized over $624 million for the inertial confinement fusion program
- The UK has committed £220 million for the STEP program
- The US NRC is developing a regulatory framework for fusion, expected to be finalized by 2027

## First Commercial Applications Will Be Niche, Not Grid-Scale: Industrial Heat and Marine Propulsion Are More Likely.

Fusion's high capital cost and technical complexity make it unlikely to compete in bulk electricity markets initially; niche applications offer a more plausible path to commercialization.

Given the cost and timeline challenges, fusion is more likely to find its first commercial applications in niche markets where its unique characteristics—high temperature heat, compact power, or zero emissions—provide a premium value. Industrial heat is a promising candidate. Many industrial processes, such as steelmaking, cement production, and chemical manufacturing, require high-temperature heat (above 500°C) that is currently supplied by fossil fuels. Fusion reactors could provide this heat directly, without the inefficiency of converting heat to electricity.

Marine propulsion is another potential early market. Large ships, such as container vessels and tankers, require significant power and are under pressure to decarbonize. Fusion reactors could provide a compact, zero-emission power source, though the engineering challenges of putting a reactor on a ship are substantial. Several fusion companies, including Helion Energy and General Fusion, have expressed interest in non-electric applications.

The DOE's ARPA-E BETHE program is funding research into alternative fusion concepts that could be more suitable for niche applications. For example, compact fusion reactors could be used for district heating or hydrogen production. These applications have lower regulatory hurdles and could be deployed sooner than grid-scale power plants.

Counter-evidence: Some argue that grid-scale electricity is the ultimate prize and that niche applications are a distraction. However, the history of energy technologies shows that many innovations first found success in niche markets before scaling. For example, solar PV was initially used for remote power and satellites before becoming a mainstream electricity source. Fusion could follow a similar trajectory.

Management implication: For corporate strategists, the niche market opportunity should not be overlooked. Companies in energy-intensive industries should explore partnerships with fusion developers to pilot industrial heat applications. Investors should consider fusion companies that have a clear path to a niche market, as these may offer earlier returns and lower risk.
