# Nuclear Fusion Will Not Be Commercially Viable Before 2035: Strategic Implications for Investors and Policymakers

Despite recent scientific breakthroughs, fusion energy faces a decade-plus path to grid parity, with private capital outpacing technical readiness and government funding remaining the primary driver.

## Key Takeaways

- Fusion grid electricity is unlikely before 2035; timelines for major projects like ITER and SPARC continue to slip. Investors should not expect fusion to compete in electricity markets within the next decade; focus on long-term optionality.
- Fusion LCOE projections remain above $50/MWh through 2040, above falling solar and wind costs. Fusion will need significant cost reductions or carbon pricing to become competitive; early applications may be in industrial heat or hydrogen.
- Private fusion investment is growing rapidly but most startups will fail; government funding remains the backbone. Portfolio diversification and public-private partnerships are critical; pure equity bets on single startups carry high risk.

## Fusion Timelines Keep Slipping: Grid Electricity Unlikely Before 2035

Major fusion projects consistently miss their milestones, pushing the realistic date for first commercial electricity beyond 2035.

The ITER project, the world's largest tokamak, has experienced multiple delays since its inception. Originally planned for first plasma in 2020, the current schedule targets 2025, with full deuterium-tritium operations not expected until 2035. Independent assessments suggest further delays are likely. The SPARC project, led by Commonwealth Fusion Systems, aims for first plasma in 2025 and net energy gain soon after, but its timeline is aggressive and unproven at scale.

The LLNL ignition milestone in December 2022 demonstrated scientific breakeven (2.05 MJ input, 3.15 MJ output), but this was a single-shot experiment using lasers, not a sustained reaction suitable for power generation. The National Ignition Facility (NIF) is not designed for continuous operation; its laser system can fire only a few times per day. Scaling to a power plant requires orders of magnitude improvement in repetition rate, efficiency, and durability.

China's CFETR (China Fusion Engineering Test Reactor) is planned for construction in the 2030s, with first plasma around 2035. While China has made rapid progress, its timeline is still more than a decade away from a demonstration reactor. No country has a publicly funded plan for a commercial fusion power plant before 2040.

Private companies like Helion Energy and TAE Technologies claim timelines as early as 2028-2030, but these projections lack independent verification and rely on unproven technologies. The Fusion Industry Association's 2023 report notes that only a handful of startups have achieved net energy gain, and none have demonstrated a sustained burn.

nuclear fusion will not achieve grid-connected electricity generation before 2035. The gap between scientific milestones and engineering reality remains wide.

Evidence:

- LLNL's 2022 experiment delivered 2.05 MJ to the target and produced 3.15 MJ of fusion energy, a scientific first but not a power plant demonstration (Source 7)
- ITER's first plasma is now scheduled for 2025, with full operations after 2035, reflecting decades of delays (Source 4)
- The DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program aims to resolve technical risks for pilot plants, but no pilot plant has been licensed yet (Source 2)

## Fusion Economics Remain Uncompetitive: LCOE Above $50/MWh Through 2040

Projected levelized cost of fusion electricity remains higher than solar and wind, even under optimistic assumptions.

The levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) for fusion is highly uncertain, with estimates ranging from $50 to over $100 per MWh by 2040. In contrast, solar PV LCOE has fallen below $30/MWh in many regions, and onshore wind is often below $40/MWh. Even with carbon pricing, fusion faces a steep cost disadvantage.

A 2021 study by the MIT Energy Initiative estimated that a first-of-a-kind fusion plant could have an LCOE of $65-100/MWh, assuming a 90% capacity factor and capital costs of $5,000-7,000/kW. However, these estimates are based on conceptual designs and do not account for the cost of tritium breeding, which remains unproven.

The DOE's Fusion Energy Sciences program acknowledges that cost competitiveness is a key challenge. The 2022 National Academies report 'Bringing Fusion to the U.S. Grid' notes that fusion must achieve LCOE below $50/MWh to be competitive with renewables and natural gas, a target that seems unlikely before 2040.

Sensitivity analyses show that fusion LCOE is most sensitive to capital cost and capacity factor. If capital costs can be reduced to $3,000/kW (similar to nuclear fission) and capacity factor maintained above 90%, LCOE could fall to $40-50/MWh. However, these assumptions are optimistic and unvalidated.

fusion LCOE will remain above $50/MWh through 2040, making it uncompetitive with solar and wind absent significant carbon pricing or technological breakthroughs.

Evidence:

- Fusion LCOE estimates from DOE and MIT range from $50 to $100/MWh by 2040, while solar and wind are already below $40/MWh (Source 1, Source 5)
- The National Academies study highlights the need for fusion LCOE below $50/MWh to compete, a target not yet within reach (Source 5)
- Capital cost assumptions for fusion are 2-3 times higher than for solar and wind, and tritium breeding costs are unknown (Source 1)

## Private Investment Surges but Most Startups Will Fail; Government Funding Remains Key

Private fusion investment is on track to exceed $10 billion cumulatively by 2025, but the majority of startups will not survive to commercialization.

According to the Fusion Industry Association, private fusion investment reached $4.8 billion cumulatively by 2023, with over $2.8 billion added in 2022 alone. Major rounds include Commonwealth Fusion Systems ($2 billion), Helion Energy ($1.7 billion), and TAE Technologies ($1.2 billion). However, the failure rate for deep-tech energy startups is high; most will not achieve technical milestones or secure follow-on funding.

Government funding remains the backbone of fusion research. The DOE's Fusion Energy Sciences budget was approximately $800 million in 2023, with additional funding from the Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program ($50 million per year). ITER's total cost is estimated at over $20 billion, funded by international partners. China's government is investing heavily in CFETR, with estimates of $10-15 billion over the next decade.

government funding remains the primary driver. Private capital is important but insufficient to overcome the technical hurdles. The DOE's public-private partnership model is designed to de-risk technologies, but the timeline remains long.

China's state-backed push is a wild card. CFETR is on an aggressive timeline, and China has a track record of rapid infrastructure deployment. If China succeeds, it could leapfrog Western efforts, with geopolitical implications for energy technology leadership.

Evidence:

- Private fusion investment reached $4.8 billion cumulatively by 2023, with projections to exceed $10 billion by 2025 (Source 6)
- DOE Fusion Energy Sciences budget was ~$800 million in 2023, with additional $50 million for milestone program (Source 1)

## First Commercial Applications Will Be Industrial Heat and Hydrogen, Not Electricity

Fusion's high-temperature output makes it suitable for industrial processes, offering an earlier path to market than grid electricity.

Several fusion companies are exploring non-electric applications. Helion Energy has announced plans to use fusion for hydrogen production, and General Fusion is targeting industrial heat for desalination and chemical processing. These applications can tolerate higher costs than grid electricity and may face less regulatory hurdles.

Industrial heat accounts for about 20% of global energy demand, and much of it is currently supplied by fossil fuels. Fusion could provide a carbon-free alternative at temperatures above 500°C, which is difficult for solar or wind to achieve. The market for industrial heat is large and growing, with potential for early adoption.

Hydrogen production via high-temperature electrolysis or thermochemical cycles could also be an early market. Fusion's continuous output and high temperature are well-suited for efficient hydrogen production. Several pilot projects are in the planning stages, though none are operational.

fusion will first be commercialized for industrial heat or hydrogen production, not electricity. This pathway reduces the need for grid integration and regulatory approval, which are major barriers for electricity generation.

However, even these applications face challenges. The cost of fusion heat must compete with natural gas and biomass. Carbon pricing could improve the economics, but the timeline remains uncertain.

Evidence:

- Helion Energy and General Fusion have announced plans for hydrogen and industrial heat applications (Source 6)
- Industrial heat accounts for ~20% of global energy demand, with high-temperature processes difficult to decarbonize (Source 3)
- Fusion's high-temperature output (>500°C) is well-suited for industrial processes and hydrogen production (Source 1)

## Fusion Will Not Materially Impact Climate Change Before 2050

Even under optimistic scenarios, fusion deployment will be too slow to significantly affect global carbon emissions before mid-century.

The IEA's Net Zero by 2050 scenario does not include fusion as a significant contributor before 2050. The IPCC's AR6 report also notes that fusion is not expected to play a major role in climate mitigation this century. The slow pace of technology development, regulatory approval, and infrastructure build-out means fusion will not be a climate solution in the near term.

fusion will not significantly impact global carbon emissions before 2050. The deployment scenarios from IEA and IPCC show fusion providing less than 1% of global electricity by 2050, even in optimistic cases.

The tritium supply constraint is a major bottleneck. Tritium is radioactive with a 12-year half-life and must be bred from lithium in the reactor. The breeding ratio is not yet proven, and lithium resources may be limited. Supply-side sizing suggests that tritium availability could limit fusion capacity to less than 100 GW by 2050, compared to global electricity demand of over 10,000 GW.

Regulatory frameworks for fusion are still in development. The U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) has not yet established a licensing framework for fusion plants, and other countries are similarly behind. The first fusion construction permit is not expected before 2030.

Public acceptance is another unknown. While fusion is generally viewed favorably, concerns about safety and waste could slow deployment. The evidence from public opinion polls is limited, but early indications are positive.

- Tritium supply constraints could limit fusion capacity to <100 GW by 2050 (Source 1)
- NRC has not yet established a licensing framework for fusion plants (Source 5)
